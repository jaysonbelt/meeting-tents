It started with two people and one trip to Mexico.

In 2002, Joey and Danielle Walker traveled from different states to serve communities in rural Mexico. They didn't know each other. They didn't have a plan beyond showing up. What they found there — the need, the resilience, the gap between what communities had and what they deserved — became the foundation for everything Meeting TENTS does today.

That origin still shapes how the organization works. Not parachuting in with prepackaged solutions. Not assuming the answer before understanding the question. Going in, listening first, and building the capacity for communities to sustain their own health outcomes long after Meeting TENTS leaves.

Children & HIV · Philippines

These children didn't choose this. Their mothers didn't know.

In the Philippines, up to 35% of children born to HIV-positive mothers are infected — not because treatment doesn't exist, but because most mothers were never tested. A test that costs less than a meal could have changed everything.

The gap isn't medical. It's knowledge.

When a mother knows her HIV status during pregnancy, the equation changes completely. With antiretroviral therapy, the transmission rate to her child drops from as high as 45% to below 5%. The medication exists. The intervention works. The barrier is getting to the test.

In the communities where Meeting TENTS works in the Philippines, the HIV epidemic is accelerating faster than almost anywhere else in the Western Pacific. But the barrier isn't access to antiretrovirals — it's the stigma, misinformation, and fear that prevent women from ever walking through the door for a test in the first place.

What Meeting TENTS does.

Meeting TENTS works with local partners — community health workers, churches, clinics, and NGOs — to bring testing closer to the communities where stigma runs highest. The model isn't about shipping in test kits. It's about building the trust, the local relationships, and the community knowledge that makes testing feel safe.

Women & HIV Testing · Philippines

Only 5% of Filipino women have ever been tested for HIV.

Not because they don't care about their health. Because the fear of what a positive result would mean — for their family, their community, their identity — is greater than the fear of the virus itself.

Why women don't get tested.

⚑
Stigma rooted in Catholic culture HIV infection is widely equated with sin and immorality. A positive test is not experienced as a medical result — it is experienced as a moral verdict. Women fear what a test would say about who they are, not just about their health.
?
Misinformation is the default Only 1 in 5 Filipino women can distinguish HIV prevention fact from local myth. In the communities where Meeting TENTS works, 97.7% of women had never been tested — and many had never heard the basic facts about transmission.
◎
No knowledge of a partner's status Women who don't know their partner's status are far less likely to seek testing. In the Philippines, the epidemic is predominantly driven by male transmission — but women bear the consequence of not knowing.
⊘
Access gaps in rural communities Testing facilities are concentrated in urban centers. In rural and lower-income communities, the distance to a clinic — combined with the social visibility of being seen seeking a test — creates a compounding barrier.

What closes the gap.

The research is clear: where stigma decreases, testing rates climb. Cambodia's testing rate is over eight times higher than the Philippines — not because Cambodian women are more health-conscious, but because decades of education and stigma reduction have changed the cultural calculus around testing.

Meeting TENTS works at the community level — with local health workers, trusted leaders, and partner organizations — to make testing feel safe, private, and normal. Not a judgment. Not an admission. An act of care.

The goal isn't just more tests. It's building the community infrastructure that makes testing the obvious choice — and sustaining it after Meeting TENTS moves on.
